Research Gap: Existing literature concentrates on California and Sun Belt cities. Midwest urban contexts - with different industrial histories, climates, and policy environments - are understudied. The Chicago MSA is the ideal natural laboratory: cross-county variation in renewable adoption creates a quasi-experimental setting.

Three Economic Channels

  • Employment generation in green technology sectors
  • Residential property value appreciation
  • New business formation in clean energy industries

Chicago MSA Advantage

  • Lake Michigan wind resources (17-20 mph at hub height)
  • FEJA (2016) + CEJA (2021) policy environment
  • 5-county variation: DuPage 21.7 installs/ZIP vs. Kane 7.4

What This Study Set Out to Answer

Research Question: How does renewable energy infrastructure development influence economic growth in the Chicago MSA, as measured by property value appreciation, clean energy employment growth, and new business formation?
OBJ 1
Property Values
Decade-long home value appreciation across 324 ZIP codes measured using Zillow ZHVI
OBJ 2
Employment
Clean energy job growth tracked at county level using BLS QCEW
OBJ 3
Business Formation
New clean energy firm registrations quantified via Census BFS

Central Hypothesis: ZIP codes and counties with greater renewable energy installations exhibit stronger economic performance across all three indicators after controlling for population density and median household income. The association is amplified in the post-CEJA period (after 2021).

Study Contribution

A longitudinal, multi-indicator, multi-county analysis filling the Midwest geographic gap. Includes interaction term (Solar x Post-CEJA) for policy-mediated effects, sub-period robustness checks separating results from the COVID housing shock, and equity analysis by income quintile.

What the Evidence Shows

✓ Positive Relationships

  • Pollin et al.: Renewables generate 16.7 jobs/$1M vs. 5.3 in fossil fuels
  • Kammen et al.: +3-4% property values near solar in CA cities
  • Dastrup et al.: 3-4% solar premium using Zillow data specifically
  • Hoen et al.: No negative property effect from wind in dense areas

✗ Negative or Null Relationships

  • Menz & Vachon: No wind-employment link at state level; temporary jobs only
  • Adelaja & McKeown: -5 to -10% property values near rural wind turbines
  • Carley et al.: Weak solar/wind correlation with GDP after controlling baseline
  • Sovacool & Watts: Benefits concentrate in high-income households

Theoretical Framework - Three Mechanisms, Three Outcomes

Roback (1982)

Spatial equilibrium: households bid up land prices for lower energy costs + green amenities - property value channel

Krugman (1991)

Agglomeration: new industries create clustering, attract skilled workers, generate increasing returns - employment channel

Porter (1990)

Cluster theory: geographic concentration accelerates entrepreneurial entry and innovation - business formation channel

Key synthesis: Benefits materialize only where industrial capacity, labor market depth, and policy institutions are sufficient. Chicago's established base positions it more favorably than rural contexts where negative effects dominate.

OLS Regression Specification

Yi = β₀ + β₁(Solari) + β₂(Windi) +
β₃(Incomei) + β₄(Densityi) +
β₅(Solari x Post-CEJAi) + εi

3 dependent variables: home value delta %, employment delta %, new firm count - N=324 ZIP codes - Robust SE - VIF 1.3-2.4

Transparency note: Wind capacity is county-level (N=5) assigned to ZIP-level regressions - a multilevel limitation acknowledged explicitly. Moran's I = 0.18 (p<0.05) indicates moderate spatial autocorrelation; spatial lag robustness checks discussed in limitations.

Robustness: Sub-Period Test

DuPage > Lake > Cook > Will > Kane rank ordering holds across all three intervals: 2015-19 (pre-pandemic), 2020-22 (shock), 2022-24. The association is not an artifact of the COVID housing boom.

Equity Dimension

Solar coefficient in bottom income quintile ZIP codes: b = 0.19 vs. MSA-wide b = 0.43. Benefits concentrate in wealthy areas - reinforcing the case for targeted low-income programs.

What It All Means

Core Finding: Solar adoption is positively and significantly associated with stronger property value appreciation, employment growth, and business formation across all specifications. Wind energy contributes primarily through labor markets (not property values). CEJA policy amplifies returns - especially for firm formation (b=0.31***). The relationship is robust to sub-period decomposition and not reducible to COVID housing shock effects.
